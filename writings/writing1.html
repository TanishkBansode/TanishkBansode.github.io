Fear - The Hidden Driver of The Human Behaviour

Description: Fear, not motivation, drives most of our actions—from daily routines to big decisions, shaping habits, industries, and even politics.

The Emotion That Drives Most People Most of the Time: Fear

It’s not motivation. It’s fear. Fear is the emotion that makes people get out of their bed, do things they don’t like, and even become highly studious.

Motivation vs. Fear

What is Motivation?
It is a force that drives individuals to do something in a certain way.

What are the things people get motivated about?

  1. Reward: I have to prepare for the interview so I’ll get a good job with good money.
  2. Fear: I have to prepare for the interview, or I’ll be jobless and disappoint my parents.

Other forces like habit and flow exist too, but they didn’t exist from the start. They stem from the above two core motivators.

Fear: The Stronger Driver

If you are a student, more than half of the A-graders in your class are likely driven by fear, not reward.

Seriously, which scenario would make you work harder?

  • Working hard so you’ll buy a car and a big house someday?
  • Or working hard so you won’t end up homeless, bullied, and hungry?

Take me as an example. When I study for a test with reward as my motivation, I often find myself daydreaming—imagining getting 100/100 and proudly showing it to my family and friends. But when my motivation is fear, imagining myself scoring poorly and disappointing my parents is so unsettling that I can’t ignore it. That discomfort forces me to focus on the task and actually study.

Politicians: The Easiest Example

Politicians may do some work throughout the year, but just before elections, it’s like they’ve taken performance-enhancing drugs!

  • They give speeches, launch schemes, and create grand plans for the future.
  • They criticize opponents and spark controversies.

Why? It’s all because of fear—fear of losing the election.

And what happens after the elections? If they win, the fear vanishes. They give a celebration speech, throw a party, and go back to NPC mode.

Fear in Our Daily Routine

What’s the first thing you do when you wake up? Get out of bed, of course. But why? Don’t you feel like sleeping all day at that moment? Yet, you still get up. Why? Fear.

Most of the things we do as part of our routine are rooted in fear. Here are some examples:

  • Brushing teeth: Fear of germs, bad breath, or yellow teeth.
  • Bathing: Fear of being unclean or smelly.
  • Sleeping: To pass the night faster and be productive again tomorrow.
  • Work/study: To avoid poverty, inferiority, or failure.

Fear is Proportional to Money

Big capitalists make massive profits by leveraging the fear of ordinary people. Take insurance companies, for example.

LIC (Life Insurance Corporation of India) is owned by the Government of India and is one of the largest insurance companies in the world. In 2023-24, LIC made a net profit of ₹40,000 crores (about $479 million)!

The Math:

  • LIC has around 290 million policyholders.
  • Only 9.5 million people die each year, and not all of them even have LIC insurance.

People give a portion of their money to these companies every month and get nothing in return 95% of the time. Yet, they feel secure just by having insurance. But are they really secure?

The Origin of LIC
After India’s independence, families often had a single breadwinner supporting 5–10 children and a spouse. If the breadwinner died, the entire family would fall into poverty. Insurance was a big deal back then, and it still is.

How Insurance Expanded
Insurance companies weren’t satisfied with life insurance alone. They started diversifying:

  • Health insurance, car insurance, bike insurance, and more.
  • They complicated the system with tiers like Gold, Platinum, and Silver. If you pick the wrong plan or forget to renew it, you might end up with nothing.

Warranty: Fear in Disguise

The same principle applies to product warranties. When we buy something expensive, like a laptop or fridge, we want it to last for a few years. Spending a little extra on a warranty feels like the safer option.

We think, “Will I regret not getting the warranty later?” For smaller items, warranties feel unnecessary, but we often pick products with them because of the fear of regret.

A Personal Thought
